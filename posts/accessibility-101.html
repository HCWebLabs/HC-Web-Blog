Accessibility 101: Why It Matters for Your Small Business

Jan 28, 2026 • Heather Cooper • —

When most people hear "web accessibility," they think it's something only big corporations need to worry about. Or they assume it means adding features for a tiny percentage of users.

Both of those assumptions are wrong.

Accessibility matters for every business, every website, and more users than you might think. Let me explain why—and what it actually means in practice.

What Is Web Accessibility?

Web accessibility means making sure your website can be used by everyone, including people with disabilities. This includes people who are blind or have low vision, deaf or hard of hearing, have mobility impairments, have cognitive disabilities, or are using older technology.

An accessible website works with screen readers (software that reads web pages aloud), keyboard-only navigation (for people who can't use a mouse), and various assistive technologies.

The Numbers
  • Over 1.3 billion people worldwide live with some form of disability—about 16% of the global population
  • In the US, 1 in 4 adults (27%) have some type of disability
  • That's roughly 61 million potential customers in America alone
  • The global spending power of people with disabilities and their families is an estimated $13 trillion
  • 70% of disabled shoppers leave websites they find difficult to use

It's Not Just About Disabilities

Here's what most people miss: accessible websites are better for everyone.

Think about it. Have you ever tried to use a website while holding a baby with one arm? Watched a video without sound because you're in a quiet office? Squinted at your phone in bright sunlight? Used voice commands while driving?

Those are all situations where accessibility features help—even for people without permanent disabilities.

Good accessibility practices lead to websites that are clearer, easier to navigate, and work better on all devices. Everyone benefits.

What Does Accessibility Look Like?

Accessibility isn't one thing—it's a bunch of small things that add up:

Accessibility Features That Matter
  • Alt text on images: Descriptions that screen readers can read aloud, so blind users know what the image shows
  • Color contrast: Text that's easy to read against its background, even for people with low vision or color blindness
  • Keyboard navigation: Being able to use Tab and Enter to navigate, for people who can't use a mouse
  • Clear headings: Properly structured content that screen readers can navigate, like chapters in a book
  • Video captions: Text versions of audio content for deaf users
  • Readable fonts: Text that's large enough and spaced well enough to read comfortably
  • Descriptive links: "Learn more about our services" instead of just "Click here"

None of these things are complicated. They're just good web design.

The Business Case

Let's talk dollars and sense. Why should a small business care about accessibility?

You're missing customers. If 1 in 4 adults has a disability, that's a huge chunk of your potential market. If they can't use your website, they'll go to a competitor who made theirs accessible.

It helps your SEO. Many accessibility practices—like proper headings, alt text, and clean code—are exactly what search engines look for. Google rewards accessible websites with better rankings.

It builds trust. When someone visits your site and it works well for them, they remember that. Accessibility shows you care about all your customers, not just some of them.

It protects you legally. Lawsuits over inaccessible websites are on the rise—and small businesses aren't immune. While I'm not a lawyer and this isn't legal advice, making your site accessible is one way to reduce that risk.

Legal Reality Check In the first half of 2025, over 2,000 ADA website accessibility lawsuits were filed—a 37% increase from the same period in 2024. Nearly 70% targeted e-commerce sites, and the majority of defendants were businesses with under $25 million in revenue. Small businesses are not exempt.

A Note on "Quick Fix" Widgets

You may have seen companies selling accessibility overlays or widgets that promise instant compliance. Be careful.

Why Widgets Aren't the Answer In 2025, over 22% of ADA lawsuits targeted websites that already had accessibility widgets installed. The FTC fined one major overlay provider $1 million for false advertising. These tools often introduce new barriers while leaving underlying issues unaddressed. Real accessibility requires proper code, not quick fixes.

What You Can Do

You don't need to become an accessibility expert overnight. But here are some places to start:

Getting Started with Accessibility
  • Test with your keyboard. Try navigating your website using only Tab, Enter, and arrow keys. Can you reach everything? Can you tell where you are on the page?
  • Check your contrast. Make sure text is easy to read. Light gray text on a white background might look sleek, but it's hard for many people to read.
  • Add alt text to images. Every image should have a description. If an image is purely decorative, you can mark it as such so screen readers skip it.
  • Use real headings. Don't just make text big and bold—use actual heading tags (H1, H2, H3) so screen readers understand the structure of your content.
  • Get an audit. If you want to know where you stand, have someone review your site specifically for accessibility issues. Many problems are easy to fix once you know they exist.

This Is Just Good Design

I want to be clear about something: accessibility isn't a burden. It's not extra work you have to do to check a box. It's simply good web design.

When I build websites, accessibility is baked in from the start. Proper HTML structure. Readable text. Working keyboard navigation. Alt text on every image. It's not an add-on—it's how websites should be built.

And the result? Websites that work better for everyone. Faster. Cleaner. Easier to use. Better for search engines. Better for business.

That's not a burden. That's just doing the job right.
